1. Purpose
Lifecycle rules define the temporal mechanics of the Prime substrate:
how artifacts enter, stabilize, persist, and dissolve.

The lifecycle ensures that all changes are:

time‑safe

deterministic

lineage‑preserving

operator‑controlled

Lifecycle is the substrate’s time geometry.

2. Lifecycle Principles
All lifecycle behavior follows four principles:

Temporal Determinism — no ambiguous transitions

Stability Before Propagation — nothing moves forward until contradictions are resolved

Lineage Continuity — every state must be traceable

Operator Primacy — the Operator defines when a phase begins or ends

Lifecycle is not workflow.
Lifecycle is ontology over time.

To deepen ontology:
Substrate Axioms

3. Canonical Lifecycle Phases
Every artifact passes through four phases:

Emergence — the artifact is declared

Stabilization — contradictions are resolved

Persistence — the artifact becomes part of the stable substrate

Dissolution — the artifact completes, retires, or is archived

These phases are universal and non‑skippable.

To deepen phase semantics:
Lifecycle Semantics

4. Phase Rules
4.1 Emergence
An artifact enters the system only when:

the Operator explicitly declares it

its scope is bound

its structural role is known

its lineage origin is established

No implicit emergence is allowed.

To expand emergence:
Instantiation Phase

4.2 Stabilization
An artifact becomes eligible for persistence only when:

contradictions are surfaced

Razor resolves them

naming is fixed

structure is coherent

lineage is validated

Stabilization produces the contradiction‑free form.

To deepen Razor mechanics:
Razor Principles

4.3 Persistence
An artifact persists when:

it has a stable identity

it participates in inheritance

it is referenced by other surfaces

it has no unresolved contradictions

Persistence is the long‑term memory of the substrate.

To deepen persistence:
Lifecycle Semantics

4.4 Dissolution
An artifact dissolves when:

it is no longer required

no active dependents exist

lineage can be cleanly reversed

the Operator explicitly authorizes dissolution

Dissolution is a first‑class lifecycle event, not deletion.

To deepen dissolution:
Resolution Phase

5. Lifecycle Enforcement
Lifecycle transitions are enforced through:

DSR‑1 — declare → scope → stabilize → propagate

lineage checks

structural invariants

operator review

No phase may be skipped.
No transition may occur implicitly.

To review DSR‑1:
DSR‑1

6. Valid and Invalid Transitions
Valid Transitions
Emergence → Stabilization

Stabilization → Persistence

Persistence → Dissolution

Invalid Transitions
Emergence → Persistence

Stabilization → Dissolution

Dissolution → Persistence

Any implicit transition

Invalid transitions must be rejected or escalated.

7. Lifecycle Invariants
The lifecycle enforces:

no artifact may skip stabilization

no artifact may persist with contradictions

no artifact may dissolve implicitly

lineage must remain continuous

Operator intent governs all transitions

These invariants maintain temporal integrity.

8. Artifact Classes
The lifecycle applies to:

structural entities (directories, files)

governance rules

axioms

lifecycle definitions

doctrine surfaces

Prime‑era historical artifacts

Each class inherits the same lifecycle, with class‑specific stabilization requirements.

9. Out of Scope
Lifecycle rules do not define:

runtime behavior

operator workflow

scanner/vessel execution semantics

TUI interactions

These belong to higher layers.
