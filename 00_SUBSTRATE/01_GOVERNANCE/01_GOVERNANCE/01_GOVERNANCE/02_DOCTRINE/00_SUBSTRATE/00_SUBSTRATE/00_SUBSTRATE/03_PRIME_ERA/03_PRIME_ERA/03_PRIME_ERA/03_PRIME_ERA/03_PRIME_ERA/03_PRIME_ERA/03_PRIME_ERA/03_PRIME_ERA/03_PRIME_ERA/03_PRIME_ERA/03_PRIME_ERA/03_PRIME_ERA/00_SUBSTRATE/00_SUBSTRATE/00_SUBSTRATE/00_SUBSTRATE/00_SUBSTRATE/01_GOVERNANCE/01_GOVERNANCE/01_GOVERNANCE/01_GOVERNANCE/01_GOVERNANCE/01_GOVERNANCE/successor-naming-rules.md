Successor Naming Rules — Prime Governance Layer (v1.0)
1. Purpose
Successor Naming Rules define the conditions, constraints, and governance mechanics for naming any successor to Xanadu Prime.
A successor is not a version, branch, or module.
A successor is a new architectural root that derives from Prime through a certified discontinuity event.

Successor naming is a rare, high‑authority governance act.

To deepen lineage:
Lineage Statement

2. Definition of a Successor
A successor is a system that:

introduces a major architectural discontinuity

cannot be expressed as a branch or extension of Prime

preserves the conceptual lineage but evolves the substrate

requires new governance, new naming, or new structural primitives

forms the second tier of the operational lineage

A successor is not a continuation.
A successor is a new beginning with inherited ancestry.

To deepen substrate rules:
Substrate Spec

3. Allowed Successor Name Forms
Successor names must follow one of the following deterministic patterns:

Prime‑Successor‑N — the Nth major architectural successor

Prime‑Successor‑Ops — operational successor layer

Prime‑Successor‑Spec — successor specification lineage

Rules:

names must preserve the Prime → Successor relationship

names must be explicit, unambiguous, and non‑overlapping

names may not encode implementation details

Naming Governance Clause

4. Conditions Required to Declare a Successor
A successor may only be named when all of the following conditions are met:

4.1 Discontinuity Event
A structural change so significant that it cannot be represented as:

a branch

a module

a revision

a governance update

The discontinuity must be structural, not stylistic.

4.2 Governance Certification
The Operator must:

declare the discontinuity

scope‑bind it

stabilize it via Razor

authorize successor naming

This is a full DSR‑1 cycle.

To deepen DSR‑1:
DSR‑1

4.3 Lineage Preservation
The successor must:

inherit the full Prime lineage

not rewrite or reinterpret Prime

not retroactively modify Prime surfaces

Lineage must remain continuous and unbroken.

4.4 Naming Stabilization
The successor name must:

pass Razor stabilization

resolve all naming contradictions

be semantically stable

4.5 Propagation
Once stabilized, the successor name must be:

added to the Prime Naming Registry

published as a lineage event

recognized as a new architectural root

To deepen registry rules:
Prime Naming Registry

5. Prohibited Successor Actions
The following actions are forbidden:

Retroactive successors — naming a successor for a past system

Implicit successors — successors must be explicitly declared

Fork successors — forks cannot self‑declare as successors

Marketing successors — names chosen for branding, not lineage

Ambiguous successors — names that obscure derivation from Prime

Successors without discontinuity — evolution is not succession

Successors must be earned, not assumed.

6. Successor Activation Rules
A successor name becomes active only when:

the discontinuity is certified

the name is stabilized

the name is added to the registry

the successor substrate exists in a running or demonstrably implementable form

Intent is insufficient.
A successor must exist.

7. Reserved Successor Names
The following names are reserved but not yet activated:

Prime‑Successor‑1 — first major architectural successor

Prime‑Successor‑Ops — operational successor layer

Prime‑Successor‑Spec — specification successor lineage

These names remain dormant until a discontinuity event is certified.

8. Successor Naming Invariants
Successor naming obeys:

no retroactive naming

no implicit lineage

no silent stabilization

no successor without discontinuity

no successor without Operator authorization

no successor that breaks Prime’s invariants

Successors must extend, not violate, Prime.

9. Out of Scope
Successor Naming Rules do not define:

implementation details

runtime behavior

public messaging

operator doctrine

versioning semantics

These belong to other governance surfaces.
